Burnout as a Systemic Issue

Addressing Leadership Pipeline Risk Through Strategic Intervention

Stephanie Janvier

MSLOC 461 - Capstone 1 Research Methods

Spring 2025

The Problem We're Exploring

Core Issue

Burnout is not an individual failure — it's a systemic breakdown arising from chronic mismatches between job design and human needs.

Key Impacts

  • Organizational: Threatens long-term sustainability and performance
  • Leadership: Weakens talent pipelines and succession planning
  • Equity: Disproportionately affects underrepresented groups
  • Individual: Damages well-being, engagement, and retention

Why This Matters Now

$8.9T Global cost of disengagement (Gallup, 2024)
40% Leaders considering stepping down due to burnout (DDI, 2025)
6% Gen Z wanting leadership roles (Deloitte, 2025)

Generational Shift

  • Gen Z and Millennials expect flexibility, inclusion, and mental health support
  • Traditional leadership models don't align with next-generation values
  • Risk of talent pipeline collapse without structural change

Important Definitions & Distinctions

Burnout (WHO Definition)

An occupational phenomenon with three dimensions:

  • Emotional Exhaustion: Feeling emotionally overextended
  • Depersonalization: Detachment from work and people
  • Reduced Accomplishment: Feeling ineffective at work

Critical Distinction

Burnout ≠ Individual Weakness
It's a structural design failure requiring systemic solutions, not just individual resilience training.

Initial Evidence

Key Research Findings

  • Structural Roots: Burnout stems from job mismatches (workload, control, rewards, fairness) - not personal failings (Leiter & Maslach, 2023)
  • Leadership Risk: Mid-career professionals face high demands with little support, driving opt-out from advancement (Mayberry, 2025)
  • Coaching Effectiveness: Evidence shows coaching reduces emotional exhaustion and improves resilience (Dyrbye et al., 2019)
  • Equity Impact: Burnout disproportionately affects women and underrepresented groups (Davis-Laack, 2014)

Six Workplace Mismatches (Leiter & Maslach)

Workload • Control • Reward • Community • Fairness • Values

What We Need to Learn

Core Research Questions

  • What early signs of burnout are most common in mid-career and high-performing professionals?
  • Which workplace mismatches are most responsible for burnout in high-stakes professions?
  • How do coaching and organizational strategies help people recover and thrive?

Why These Questions Matter

Understanding burnout patterns and effective interventions will help organizations redesign systems to retain talent and build sustainable leadership pipelines.

Research Approach

Quantitative Survey

  • Sample: 2+ years experience in healthcare, law, public service
  • Focus: Mid-career professionals in leadership/senior roles
  • Measures: Burnout dimensions, workplace mismatches, support access

Qualitative Interviews

  • Participants: Leaders who've experienced/managed burnout
  • Focus: Intervention experiences, equity perspectives
  • Duration: 45+ minutes per interview

Target Timeline

  • Weeks 1-2: Finalize instruments, distribute survey
  • Weeks 2-4: Conduct interviews, begin analysis
  • Week 5: Synthesize insights for design recommendations

Expected Impact & Next Steps

Anticipated Findings

  • Clear burnout patterns in mid-career professionals
  • Identification of most critical workplace mismatches
  • Evidence-based intervention recommendations
  • Framework for equity-centered burnout prevention

Proposed Interventions

  • Role Redesign: Using six mismatches framework
  • Coaching Programs: For mid-career and high-performers
  • Pipeline Rebuilding: Equity-centered succession planning

Goal: Transform burnout from inevitable to preventable
